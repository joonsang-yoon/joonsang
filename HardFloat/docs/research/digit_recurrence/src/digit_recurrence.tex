\title{Digit-Recurrence Division and Square Root\\[0.3em]
\large A polished, self-contained tutorial}
\author{}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Digit-recurrence algorithms compute a quotient or a square root one digit at a time while
maintaining a scaled residual.  Their hardware appeal comes from redundant signed-digit sets:
because adjacent digits have overlapping validity regions, the next digit can be selected from
truncated information rather than from an exact, full-width residual.

This tutorial develops the method from first principles and keeps the algebra explicit.  We begin
with the general notion of redundancy, derive the exact recurrences for division and square root,
prove the residual containment conditions, and then show how those exact inequalities are converted
into practical lookup-table selectors driven by truncated operands.  Detailed Radix-2 and Radix-4
examples are worked out for both division and square root, including the determination of the
precision parameters \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\), the treatment of positive and negative divisors,
and the startup exceptions that appear in the square-root case.  The last part explains how the
overlap cells used by the ``basic'' selectors can be resolved deterministically to obtain the
``optimized'' selectors shown in the accompanying figures.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Division and square root are among the slowest elementary arithmetic operations in hardware.
Unlike addition, subtraction, and many multiplication architectures, they are naturally iterative.
Digit-recurrence algorithms attack the problem by generating the result from most significant digit
to least significant digit.  At each iteration they do two things:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item choose the next quotient digit \(q_{j+1}\) or root digit \(s_{j+1}\), and
  \item update a scaled residual \(R_{j+1}\).
\end{enumerate}

The critical path is the digit selector.  A naive selector would need the exact residual at full
precision, which in turn would require a full-width carry-propagating addition every cycle.  SRT
algorithms avoid this by using a redundant digit set.  Because adjacent digits overlap in their
validity regions, the selector may safely use only a truncated estimate of the residual (and, for
square root, a truncated estimate of the partial root as well).  In practical hardware, the
residual can therefore stay in carry-save form and only a short carry-propagate adder is needed in
front of a small PLA or lookup table.

Two normalization choices are used throughout this tutorial:
\begin{align*}
  \text{division: } &N \in \paren{0,\frac14}, \qquad D \in \brak{\frac12,1},\\
  \text{square root: } &X \in \brak{\frac14,1}.
\end{align*}
These are slightly tighter than the broadest possible SRT assumptions, but they make the startup
logic much cleaner.  The mathematics of the recurring loop is unchanged; only the bookkeeping for
the first step becomes simpler.

The tutorial uses two complementary views of digit selection.

\paragraph{Exact view.}
The recurrence is analyzed over the real-valued residual \(R_j\) and the exact divisor \(D\) or
partial root \(S_j\).  This yields ideal selection intervals.

\paragraph{Truncated view.}
Hardware does not see the exact residual.  Instead it sees a truncated residual estimate \(t_j\),
converted to an integer \(\tau_j\), together with a truncated estimate of the divisor or partial
root.  The selector therefore acts on a finite grid:
\[
  (\delta,\tau_j) \quad \text{for division}, \qquad (\sigma_j,\tau_j) \quad \text{for square root}.
\]
The Python scripts in the repository enumerate this finite grid and plot the cells that admit one or
more legal digits.  The formulas given later for the ``basic'' selectors are compact descriptions of
those enumerated cells.

\section{Redundant digit sets and overlap}
\label{sec:redundancy}

For radix \(B\), SRT-style digit recurrence selects every result digit from the symmetric set
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:redundancy:digit_set}
  q_{j+1}, s_{j+1} \in \set{-a,-a+1,\dotsc,a-1,a}.
\end{equation}
The redundancy factor is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:redundancy:rho}
  \rho = \frac{a}{B-1}.
\end{equation}

The key requirement is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:redundancy:overlap}
  \rho > \frac12.
\end{equation}
Why?  Later we will derive intervals of the form
\[
  L_k \le B R_j \le U_k
\]
that say when digit \(k\) is legal.  Adjacent digits \(k\) and \(k+1\) overlap exactly when
\(U_k \ge L_{k+1}\).  That inequality reduces to \(\rho \ge 1/2\), and the strict inequality
\(\rho > 1/2\) gives a nonzero overlap width.  That nonzero overlap is what allows the selector to
work from truncated information: if the exact residual lies near a boundary, either of the two
adjacent digits can still keep the next residual inside the legal containment range.

Typical SRT designs choose
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:redundancy:range}
  \frac12 < \rho \le 1,
\end{equation}
equivalently
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:redundancy:range_a}
  \frac{B-1}{2} < a \le B-1.
\end{equation}
The upper end \(\rho=1\) is maximally redundant.  Greater redundancy means wider overlap and easier
selection, but it also means more multiples of the divisor (or of the partial root) must be formed.
The designer therefore trades selector simplicity against datapath complexity.

\section{Division}
\label{sec:division}

\subsection{Normalized problem and quotient expansion}
\label{ssec:division:setup}

We start from
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:fundamental}
  N = DQ + r,
\end{equation}
where \(N\) is the dividend, \(D\) the divisor, \(Q\) the exact quotient, and \(r\) the final
remainder.

With the tutorial normalization
\[
  N \in \paren{0,\frac14}, \qquad D \in \brak{\frac12,1},
\]
the exact quotient satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:q_range}
  0 < \frac{N}{D} < \frac{\frac14}{\frac12} = \frac12.
\end{equation}
This is why we can safely start with \(Q_0=0\) and choose the first digit from the nonnegative half
of the digit set.

The quotient approximation after \(j\) digits is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:Qj_def}
  Q_j = Q_0 + \sum_{i=0}^{j-1} q_{i+1} B^{-(i+1)}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:Qj_update}
  Q_{j+1} = Q_j + q_{j+1} B^{-(j+1)}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Tail bound and residual containment}
\label{ssec:division:containment}

Write the exact quotient as the infinite signed-digit expansion
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:Q_infinite}
  \frac{N}{D}
  = Q_0 + \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} q_{i+1} B^{-(i+1)}.
\end{equation}
Subtract \cref{eq:division:Qj_def} from \cref{eq:division:Q_infinite}.  The approximation error after
iteration \(j\) is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:error_def}
  \varepsilon_j
  = \frac{N}{D} - Q_j
  = \sum_{i=j}^{\infty} q_{i+1} B^{-(i+1)}.
\end{equation}
Because every digit satisfies \(\abs{q_{i+1}} \le a\),
\begin{align}
  \abs{\varepsilon_j}
  &= \abs{\sum_{i=j}^{\infty} q_{i+1} B^{-(i+1)}} \notag\\
  &\le \sum_{i=j}^{\infty} \abs{q_{i+1}} B^{-(i+1)} \notag\\
  &\le \sum_{i=j}^{\infty} a B^{-(i+1)} \notag\\
  &= a B^{-(j+1)} \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} B^{-m} \label{eq:division:error_geometric_1}\\
  &= a B^{-(j+1)} \cdot \frac{1}{1-B^{-1}} \notag\\
  &= \frac{a}{B-1} B^{-j} \cdot B^{-1} \notag\\
  &= \rho B^{-j}. \label{eq:division:error_geometric_2}
\end{align}
Replacing \(j\) with \(j+1\) to match the selector indexing used later,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:error_bound}
  \abs{\frac{N}{D} - Q_{j+1}} \le \rho B^{-(j+1)}.
\end{equation}

Multiply \cref{eq:division:error_bound} by the positive quantity \(B^{j+1}D\):
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:scaled_error}
  \abs{B^{j+1}(N-DQ_{j+1})} \le \rho D.
\end{equation}
This motivates the scaled residual
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:R_def}
  R_{j+1} = B^{j+1}(N-DQ_{j+1}),
\end{equation}
so the containment condition becomes
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:containment}
  \abs{R_{j+1}} \le \rho D.
\end{equation}
At the end of the computation,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:final_remainder}
  R_n = B^n (N-DQ_n) = B^n r.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Residual recurrence}
\label{ssec:division:recurrence}

Start from the definition of \(R_{j+1}\):
\begin{align}
  R_{j+1}
  &= B^{j+1}\paren{N-DQ_{j+1}} \notag\\
  &= B^{j+1}\paren{N-D\paren{Q_j + q_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)}}} \label{eq:division:recurrence_step_1}\\
  &= B^{j+1}\paren{N-DQ_j} - q_{j+1}D \notag\\
  &= B \cdot B^j \paren{N-DQ_j} - q_{j+1}D \notag\\
  &= BR_j - q_{j+1}D. \label{eq:division:recurrence}
\end{align}
So each cycle multiplies the old residual by the radix, subtracts the selected digit times the
divisor, and must leave the new residual inside \cref{eq:division:containment}.

\subsection{Exact digit-selection intervals}
\label{ssec:division:exact_selection}

\subsubsection{Positive divisor}
\label{sssec:division:positive_divisor}

For \(D>0\), \cref{eq:division:containment} is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:containment_positive}
  -\rho D \le R_{j+1} \le \rho D.
\end{equation}
Insert \cref{eq:division:recurrence}:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:containment_positive_sub}
  -\rho D \le BR_j - q_{j+1}D \le \rho D.
\end{equation}
Add \(q_{j+1}D\) throughout:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:positive_interval}
  \paren{q_{j+1}-\rho}D \le BR_j \le \paren{q_{j+1}+\rho}D.
\end{equation}
So, for a fixed positive divisor, digit \(k\) is legal exactly when
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:positive_interval_k}
  (k-\rho)D \le BR_j \le (k+\rho)D.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Negative divisor}
\label{sssec:division:negative_divisor}

For \(D<0\), \(\abs{D}=-D\), so \cref{eq:division:containment} becomes
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:containment_negative}
  \rho D \le R_{j+1} \le -\rho D.
\end{equation}
Again insert \cref{eq:division:recurrence}:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:containment_negative_sub}
  \rho D \le BR_j - q_{j+1}D \le -\rho D.
\end{equation}
Add \(q_{j+1}D\) to every term:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:negative_interval}
  (q_{j+1}+\rho)D \le BR_j \le (q_{j+1}-\rho)D.
\end{equation}
The endpoints appear reversed simply because \(D\) is negative.

\subsection{Initialization}
\label{ssec:division:init}

At \(j=0\), the approximation error bound is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:init_error}
  \abs{\frac{N}{D} - Q_1} \le \rho B^{-1}.
\end{equation}
This is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:init_interval}
  Q_1 - \rho B^{-1} \le \frac{N}{D} \le Q_1 + \rho B^{-1}.
\end{equation}
With \(Q_0=0\) and \(Q_1 = q_1 B^{-1}\), the first digit must cover the entire interval
\((0,\frac12)\) from \cref{eq:division:q_range}.  It is enough that
\begin{align}
  Q_0 - \rho B^{-1} &\le 0, \label{eq:division:init_cover_1}\\
  \paren{Q_0 + aB^{-1}} + \rho B^{-1} &\ge \frac12. \label{eq:division:init_cover_2}
\end{align}
The first inequality is immediate because \(Q_0=0\).  In the second,
\[
  aB^{-1} + \rho B^{-1}
  = \frac{a+\rho}{B}
  = \frac{\rho(B-1)+\rho}{B}
  = \rho.
\]
Therefore \cref{eq:division:init_cover_2} reduces to \(\rho \ge \frac12\), which holds strictly in
the redundant case.

The concrete startup intervals for the worked examples are:

\paragraph{Radix-2 \((B=2,\rho=1)\).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item If \(q_1=0\), then \(Q_1=0\) and
    \[
      -\frac12 \le \frac{N}{D} \le \frac12
      \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
      -\frac12 D \le N \le \frac12 D.
    \]
  \item If \(q_1=1\), then \(Q_1=\frac12\) and
    \[
      0 \le \frac{N}{D} \le 1
      \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
      0 \le N \le D.
    \]
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Radix-4 \((B=4,\rho=\frac23)\).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item If \(q_1=0\), then \(Q_1=0\) and
    \[
      -\frac16 \le \frac{N}{D} \le \frac16
      \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
      -\frac16 D \le N \le \frac16 D.
    \]
  \item If \(q_1=1\), then \(Q_1=\frac14\) and
    \[
      \frac1{12} \le \frac{N}{D} \le \frac5{12}
      \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
      \frac1{12} D \le N \le \frac5{12} D.
    \]
  \item If \(q_1=2\), then \(Q_1=\frac12\) and
    \[
      \frac13 \le \frac{N}{D} \le \frac23
      \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
      \frac13 D \le N \le \frac23 D.
    \]
\end{itemize}

\subsection{From exact intervals to a truncated selector}
\label{ssec:division:truncated_selector}

Hardware does not carry the exact value \(BR_j\) into the selector.  Instead it keeps the residual
in carry-save form and forms a short carry-propagated truncation.  Let
\[
  BR_j = Y_j + Z_j,
\]
with \(Y_j\) the carry-save sum word and \(Z_j\) the carry word.  Keep only \(\alpha\) fractional
bits, add those retained bits with a small CPA, and call the result \(t_j\).  The one-sided
truncation property is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:trunc_error_generic}
  0 \le BR_j - t_j < 2^{-(\alpha-1)}.
\end{equation}
For division we also truncate the divisor to \(\beta\) fractional bits.

The selector itself does not operate on fixed-point fractions; it operates on integers.  Hence we
define
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:division:tau_delta_generic}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{2^\alpha t_j},\\
  \delta &= \Int\paren{2^\beta D_{\text{trunc}}}.
\end{align}
Once \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are chosen, \((\delta,\tau_j)\) lies on a finite grid.  Each integer
pair corresponds to a small rectangle in the exact \((D,BR_j)\) plane.  A digit is legal for that
cell if some point in the cell satisfies the exact selector inequalities.  The repository's Python
scripts enumerate those cells and draw the legal regions.  The table equations given later are just
closed-form descriptions of those enumerated regions.

Before drawing the table, we still need a \emph{continuity condition}: after truncation, adjacent
digit regions must continue to overlap.  That condition determines the minimum practical values of
\(\alpha\) and \(\beta\).

\paragraph{Positive divisor continuity.}
For adjacent digits \(k-1\) and \(k\), define the exact upper and lower boundaries
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:UkLk_positive}
  U_{k-1}(D) = (k-1+\rho)D,\qquad L_k(D) = (k-\rho)D.
\end{equation}
Residual truncation can reduce the usable upper boundary by at most \(2^{-(\alpha-1)}\), so a
sufficient overlap condition is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:continuity_positive}
  \min_D\!\big(U_{k-1}(D) - 2^{-(\alpha-1)}\big)
  \;\ge\;
  \max_D L_k(D).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Negative divisor continuity.}
For \(D<0\), the roles of the two boundary formulas reverse:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:UkLk_negative}
  U_k(D) = (k-\rho)D,\qquad L_{k-1}(D) = (k-1+\rho)D.
\end{equation}
The sufficient continuity condition is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:continuity_negative}
  \min_D\!\big(U_k(D) - 2^{-(\alpha-1)}\big)
  \;\ge\;
  \max_D L_{k-1}(D).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Worked example: Radix-2 division}
\label{ssec:division:radix2}

Take \(B=2\) and the maximally redundant digit set \(\{-1,0,1\}\).  Then \(a=1\) and
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix2_rho}
  \rho = \frac{1}{2-1} = 1.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Exact selection intervals}
\label{sssec:division:radix2_exact}

Substitute \(B=2\) and \(\rho=1\) into \cref{eq:division:positive_interval_k} and
\cref{eq:division:negative_interval}.

For \(D>0\),
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix2_positive_exact}
  q_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    1  & \text{if } 0 \le 2R_j \le 2D,\\
    0  & \text{if } -D \le 2R_j \le D,\\
    -1 & \text{if } -2D \le 2R_j \le 0.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

For \(D<0\),
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix2_negative_exact}
  q_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    1  & \text{if } 2D \le 2R_j \le 0,\\
    0  & \text{if } D \le 2R_j \le -D,\\
    -1 & \text{if } 0 \le 2R_j \le -2D.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Choosing \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\)}
\label{sssec:division:radix2_precision}

For the practical selector we use
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:division:radix2_tau_delta_defs}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{2^\alpha t_j},
  & \tau_j &\le 2^{\alpha+1} R_j < \tau_j + 2,\\
  \delta &= \Int\paren{2^\beta D_{\text{trunc}}},
  & \delta &\le 2^\beta D < \delta + 1 \qquad (D>0).
\end{align}
The smallest useful divisor truncation for a two-bin positive divisor is \(\beta=2\), so
\(\delta \in \{2,3\}\).

Now apply the continuity condition.

\paragraph{Boundary between \(q=0\) and \(q=1\).}
Here \(k=1\).  Since \(\rho=1\),
\[
  U_0(D) = (0+1)D = D,\qquad L_1(D) = (1-1)D = 0.
\]
Insert these into \cref{eq:division:continuity_positive}:
\begin{align}
  \min_D \paren{D - 2^{-(\alpha-1)}} &\ge 0 \notag\\
  \frac{\delta}{2^\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} &\ge 0. \label{eq:division:radix2_boundary_01}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Boundary between \(q=-1\) and \(q=0\).}
Here \(k=0\).  Again \(\rho=1\), so
\[
  U_{-1}(D) = (-1+1)D = 0,\qquad L_0(D) = (0-1)D = -D.
\]
Insert these into \cref{eq:division:continuity_positive}:
\begin{align}
  0 - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} &\ge -D \notag\\
  D &\ge 2^{-(\alpha-1)} \notag\\
  \frac{\delta}{2^\beta} &\ge 2^{-(\alpha-1)}. \label{eq:division:radix2_boundary_m10}
\end{align}

The worst case is the smallest \(\delta\), namely \(\delta_{\min}=2^{\beta-1}\).  With \(\beta=2\),
\cref{eq:division:radix2_boundary_01,eq:division:radix2_boundary_m10} both reduce to
\[
  \frac12 \ge 2^{-(\alpha-1)},
\]
hence the smallest admissible choice is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix2_alpha_beta}
  \alpha = 2,\qquad \beta = 2.
\end{equation}

With that choice,
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:division:radix2_tau_delta_values}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{4\,t_j},
  & \tau_j &\le 8R_j < \tau_j + 2,\\
  \delta &= \Int\paren{4 D_{\text{trunc}}},
  & \delta &\in \set{2,3}.
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Positive-divisor selector}
\label{sssec:division:radix2_positive_selector}

Enumerating the finite grid \((\delta,\tau_j)\) and marking every legal digit cell yields the basic
positive-divisor selector
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix2_positive_selector}
  q_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    1  & \text{if } 0 \le \tau_j < 2(\delta+1),\\
    0  & \text{if } -\delta \le \tau_j \le \delta - 2,\\
    -1 & \text{if } -2(\delta+1)-2 < \tau_j \le -2.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Cells on the boundaries belong to two exact digit regions; the plot marks those cells as overlap
cells.

\subsubsection{Negative-divisor selector}
\label{sssec:division:radix2_negative_selector}

For \(D<0\) we define
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:division:radix2_negative_tau_delta}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{4\,t_j},
  & \tau_j &\le 8R_j < \tau_j + 2,\\
  \delta &= \Int\paren{4 D_{\text{trunc}}},
  & \delta &\in \set{-4,-3}.
\end{align}
The continuity algebra is the sign-reflected version of the positive-divisor case, so the same
choice \(\alpha=2,\beta=2\) remains valid.  Enumerating the \((\delta,\tau_j)\) grid gives
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix2_negative_selector}
  q_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    1  & \text{if } 2\delta - 2 < \tau_j \le -2,\\
    0  & \text{if } \delta + 1 \le \tau_j \le -(\delta+1)-2,\\
    -1 & \text{if } 0 \le \tau_j \le -2\delta.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Worked example: Radix-4 division}
\label{ssec:division:radix4}

Now take \(B=4\) with the minimally redundant digit set \(\{-2,-1,0,1,2\}\).  Then \(a=2\) and
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix4_rho}
  \rho = \frac{2}{4-1} = \frac23.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Exact selection intervals}
\label{sssec:division:radix4_exact}

For \(D>0\), substitute \(B=4\) and \(\rho=2/3\) into \cref{eq:division:positive_interval_k}:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix4_positive_exact}
  q_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    2  & \text{if } \frac43 D \le 4R_j \le \frac83 D,\\
    1  & \text{if } \frac13 D \le 4R_j \le \frac53 D,\\
    0  & \text{if } -\frac23 D \le 4R_j \le \frac23 D,\\
    -1 & \text{if } -\frac53 D \le 4R_j \le -\frac13 D,\\
    -2 & \text{if } -\frac83 D \le 4R_j \le -\frac43 D.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
For \(D<0\),
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix4_negative_exact}
  q_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    2  & \text{if } \frac83 D \le 4R_j \le \frac43 D,\\
    1  & \text{if } \frac53 D \le 4R_j \le \frac13 D,\\
    0  & \text{if } \frac23 D \le 4R_j \le -\frac23 D,\\
    -1 & \text{if } -\frac13 D \le 4R_j \le -\frac53 D,\\
    -2 & \text{if } -\frac43 D \le 4R_j \le -\frac83 D.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Choosing \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\)}
\label{sssec:division:radix4_precision}

Use
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:division:radix4_tau_delta_defs}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{2^\alpha t_j},
  & \tau_j &\le 2^{\alpha+2}R_j < \tau_j + 2,\\
  \delta &= \Int\paren{2^\beta D_{\text{trunc}}},
  & \delta &\le 2^\beta D < \delta + 1 \qquad (D>0).
\end{align}
The selector used in the scripts retains \(\beta=4\) fractional divisor bits, so
\(\delta \in \{8,9,\dotsc,15\}\).

Apply \cref{eq:division:continuity_positive} to each adjacent digit boundary.

\paragraph{Boundary between \(q=1\) and \(q=2\).}
Here \(k=2\), so
\[
  U_1(D) = \paren{1+\frac23}D = \frac53 D,\qquad
  L_2(D) = \paren{2-\frac23}D = \frac43 D.
\]
Therefore
\begin{align}
  \min_D\paren{\frac53 D - 2^{-(\alpha-1)}} &\ge \max_D \paren{\frac43 D} \notag\\
  \frac53 \frac{\delta}{2^\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} &\ge \frac43 \frac{\delta+1}{2^\beta} \notag\\
  \frac13 2^{-\beta}\delta - \frac43 2^{-\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} &\ge 0.
  \label{eq:division:radix4_boundary_12}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Boundary between \(q=0\) and \(q=1\).}
Now \(k=1\), so
\[
  U_0(D) = \frac23 D,\qquad L_1(D)=\frac13 D.
\]
Hence
\begin{align}
  \frac23 \frac{\delta}{2^\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)}
  &\ge
  \frac13 \frac{\delta+1}{2^\beta} \notag\\
  \frac13 2^{-\beta}\delta - \frac13 2^{-\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} &\ge 0.
  \label{eq:division:radix4_boundary_01}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Boundary between \(q=-1\) and \(q=0\).}
Now \(k=0\), so
\[
  U_{-1}(D)= -\frac13 D,\qquad L_0(D) = -\frac23 D.
\]
The same algebra gives
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix4_boundary_m10}
  \frac13 2^{-\beta}\delta - \frac13 2^{-\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} \ge 0.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Boundary between \(q=-2\) and \(q=-1\).}
Now \(k=-1\), so
\[
  U_{-2}(D) = -\frac43 D,\qquad L_{-1}(D) = -\frac53 D.
\]
Hence
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix4_boundary_m2m1}
  \frac13 2^{-\beta}\delta - \frac43 2^{-\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} \ge 0.
\end{equation}

The restrictive inequalities are \cref{eq:division:radix4_boundary_12,eq:division:radix4_boundary_m2m1}.
At the worst-case divisor bucket \(\delta_{\min}=8\) and \(\beta=4\),
\[
  \frac13 2^{-4}\cdot 8 - \frac43 2^{-4} = \frac16 - \frac1{12} = \frac1{12}.
\]
So we need
\[
  \frac1{12} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} \ge 0.
\]
The smallest integer \(\alpha\) satisfying this is \(\alpha=5\), because
\[
  2^{-(5-1)} = \frac1{16} \le \frac1{12},
  \qquad
  2^{-(4-1)} = \frac18 > \frac1{12}.
\]
Thus the design point used in the scripts is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix4_alpha_beta}
  \alpha = 5,\qquad \beta = 4.
\end{equation}

With that choice,
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:division:radix4_tau_delta_values}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{32\,t_j},
  & \tau_j &\le 128R_j < \tau_j + 2,\\
  \delta &= \Int\paren{16D_{\text{trunc}}},
  & \delta &\in \set{8,\dotsc,15}.
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Positive-divisor selector}
\label{sssec:division:radix4_positive_selector}

Enumerating the finite grid and fitting the resulting regions gives
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:division:radix4_positive_selector}
  q_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    2  & \text{if } \frac83(\delta+1) \le \tau_j < \frac{16}{3}(\delta+1),\\
    1  & \text{if } \frac23(\delta+1) \le \tau_j \le \frac{10}{3}\delta - 2,\\
    0  & \text{if } -\frac43\delta \le \tau_j \le \frac43\delta - 2,\\
    -1 & \text{if } -\frac{10}{3}\delta \le \tau_j \le -\frac23(\delta+1) - 2,\\
    -2 & \text{if } -\frac{16}{3}(\delta+1)-2 < \tau_j \le -\frac83(\delta+1)-2.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Negative-divisor selector}
\label{sssec:division:radix4_negative_selector}

For \(D<0\), use

  \label{eq:division:radix4_negative_tau_delta}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{32\,t_j},
  & \tau_j &\le 128R_j < \tau_j + 2,\\

    0  & \text{if } \frac43(\delta+1) \le \tau_j \le -\frac43(\delta+1) - 2,\\
    -1 & \text{if } -\frac23\delta \le \tau_j \le -\frac{10}{3}(\delta+1) - 2,\\
    -2 & \text{if } -\frac83\delta \le \tau_j \le -\frac{16}{3}\delta.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Basic versus optimized division selectors}
\label{ssec:division:optimized}

The \emph{basic} selectors leave the overlap cells explicit: a cell may carry two legal digits, for
example \(\{0,1\}\) or \(\{-1,0\}\).  This is useful pedagogically because it makes the overlap
visible.  A hardware implementation usually goes one step further and assigns every overlap cell to a
single digit.  The optimized selectors used by the repository's Radix-4 scripts do exactly that.

The assignment is not arbitrary.  Among all possible split points inside an overlap interval, the
scripts choose split points aligned to convenient modulo classes (modulo \(4\), then \(8\), and then
\(16\) as the overlap becomes wider).  That alignment tends to simplify the resulting Boolean logic.
The exact boundary-snap rule is given in \cref{sec:appendix_transform}.  The important point for the
main text is that the optimized selector never changes the exact legality of the recurrence; it only
chooses one digit deterministically where the basic selector would have shown two.

\section{Square root}
\label{sec:sqrt}

\subsection{Normalized problem and root expansion}
\label{ssec:sqrt:setup}

The square-root problem is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:fundamental}
  X = S^2 + r,
\end{equation}
where \(X\) is the radicand, \(S = \sqrt{X}\) is the exact root, and \(r\) is the final remainder.

With the tutorial normalization \(X \in [\frac14,1)\), the exact root lies in
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:root_range}
  \frac12 \le \sqrt{X} < 1.
\end{equation}
This is why we can start from \(S_0=1\): the initial approximation begins above the exact root and
the selected digits bring it down toward the correct value.

The partial root after \(j\) digits is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:Sj_def}
  S_j = S_0 + \sum_{i=0}^{j-1} s_{i+1} B^{-(i+1)},
\end{equation}
so
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:Sj_update}
  S_{j+1} = S_j + s_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Tail bound and residual containment}
\label{ssec:sqrt:containment}

Write the exact root as the infinite signed-digit expansion
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:S_infinite}
  \sqrt{X} = S_0 + \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} s_{i+1} B^{-(i+1)}.
\end{equation}
Subtract \cref{eq:sqrt:Sj_def} to obtain the error
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:error_def}
  e_j = \sqrt{X} - S_j = \sum_{i=j}^{\infty} s_{i+1} B^{-(i+1)}.
\end{equation}
Exactly the same geometric-series bound used for division gives
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:error_bound}
  \abs{e_{j+1}} = \abs{\sqrt{X} - S_{j+1}} \le \rho B^{-(j+1)}.
\end{equation}

Now use the identity
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:factorization}
  X - S_{j+1}^2
  = \paren{\sqrt{X} - S_{j+1}}\paren{\sqrt{X} + S_{j+1}}
  = e_{j+1}\paren{2S_{j+1} + e_{j+1}}.
\end{equation}
Because \(e_{j+1}\) may be positive or negative, we bound both sides separately.

\paragraph{Upper bound.}
From \(\abs{e_{j+1}} \le \rho B^{-(j+1)}\) we have \(e_{j+1} \le \rho B^{-(j+1)}\).  Also
\(2S_{j+1} + e_{j+1} \le 2S_{j+1} + \rho B^{-(j+1)}\).  Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:upper_bound_pre}
  X - S_{j+1}^2 \le \rho B^{-(j+1)}\paren{2S_{j+1} + \rho B^{-(j+1)}}.
\end{equation}
Expand the right-hand side:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:upper_bound_expanded}
  X - S_{j+1}^2 \le 2\rho S_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)} + \rho^2 B^{-2(j+1)}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Lower bound.}
Likewise \(e_{j+1} \ge -\rho B^{-(j+1)}\), and
\[
  2S_{j+1} + e_{j+1} \ge 2S_{j+1} - \rho B^{-(j+1)}.
\]
So
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:lower_bound_pre}
  X - S_{j+1}^2 \ge -\rho B^{-(j+1)}\paren{2S_{j+1} - \rho B^{-(j+1)}}
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:lower_bound_expanded}
  X - S_{j+1}^2 \ge -2\rho S_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)} + \rho^2 B^{-2(j+1)}.
\end{equation}

Collecting \cref{eq:sqrt:upper_bound_expanded,eq:sqrt:lower_bound_expanded},
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:containment_pre}
  \rho^2 B^{-2(j+1)} - 2\rho S_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)}
  \le X - S_{j+1}^2 \le
  \rho^2 B^{-2(j+1)} + 2\rho S_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)}.
\end{equation}

Define the scaled residual
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:R_def}
  R_{j+1} = B^{j+1}\paren{X - S_{j+1}^2}.
\end{equation}
Multiply \cref{eq:sqrt:containment_pre} by \(B^{j+1}\) to obtain the containment condition
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:containment}
  \rho^2 B^{-(j+1)} - 2\rho S_{j+1}
  \le R_{j+1} \le
  \rho^2 B^{-(j+1)} + 2\rho S_{j+1}.
\end{equation}
At the end,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:final_remainder}
  R_n = B^n\paren{X-S_n^2} = B^n r.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Residual recurrence}
\label{ssec:sqrt:recurrence}

Start from \cref{eq:sqrt:R_def} and use \cref{eq:sqrt:Sj_update}:
\begin{align}
  R_{j+1}
  &= B^{j+1}\paren{X-S_{j+1}^2} \notag\\
  &= B^{j+1}\paren{X-\paren{S_j + s_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)}}^2} \label{eq:sqrt:recurrence_step_1}\\
  &= B^{j+1}\paren{X - S_j^2 - 2S_j s_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)} - s_{j+1}^2 B^{-2(j+1)}} \notag\\
  &= B^{j+1}\paren{X-S_j^2} - 2S_j s_{j+1} - s_{j+1}^2 B^{-(j+1)} \notag\\
  &= BR_j - s_{j+1}\paren{2S_j + s_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)}}. \label{eq:sqrt:recurrence}
\end{align}

\subsection{Exact digit-selection intervals}
\label{ssec:sqrt:exact_selection}

Insert \cref{eq:sqrt:recurrence} into \cref{eq:sqrt:containment}.  The left-hand side becomes
\begin{align*}
  \rho^2 B^{-(j+1)} - 2\rho S_{j+1}
  &=
  \rho^2 B^{-(j+1)} - 2\rho\paren{S_j + s_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)}}\\
  &=
  -2\rho S_j + \paren{\rho^2 - 2\rho s_{j+1}}B^{-(j+1)}.
\end{align*}
Move the subtraction term from \cref{eq:sqrt:recurrence} to the left:
\begin{align}
  BR_j
  &\ge
  s_{j+1}\paren{2S_j + s_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)}}
  + \rho^2 B^{-(j+1)} - 2\rho\paren{S_j + s_{j+1}B^{-(j+1)}} \notag\\
  &=
  2(s_{j+1}-\rho)S_j + \paren{s_{j+1}^2 - 2\rho s_{j+1} + \rho^2}B^{-(j+1)} \notag\\
  &=
  2(s_{j+1}-\rho)S_j + \paren{s_{j+1}-\rho}^2 B^{-(j+1)}. \label{eq:sqrt:lower_selector}
\end{align}
Exactly the same rearrangement on the upper side gives
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:upper_selector}
  BR_j \le 2(s_{j+1}+\rho)S_j + \paren{s_{j+1}+\rho}^2 B^{-(j+1)}.
\end{equation}
So the exact selection interval is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:exact_interval}
  2(s_{j+1}-\rho)S_j + \paren{s_{j+1}-\rho}^2 B^{-(j+1)}
  \le BR_j \le
  2(s_{j+1}+\rho)S_j + \paren{s_{j+1}+\rho}^2 B^{-(j+1)}.
\end{equation}

This is the crucial difference from division: the interval boundaries depend on \(S_j\), and they also
contain explicit \(B^{-(j+1)}\) terms.  That extra iteration dependence is why square-root selectors
need more careful startup treatment.

\subsection{Initialization}
\label{ssec:sqrt:init}

At \(j=0\), the error bound is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:init_error}
  \abs{\sqrt{X} - S_1} \le \rho B^{-1},
\end{equation}
that is,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:init_interval}
  S_1 - \rho B^{-1} \le \sqrt{X} \le S_1 + \rho B^{-1}.
\end{equation}
With \(S_0=1\) and \(S_1 = 1 + s_1 B^{-1}\), the first digit must cover the full interval
\([\frac12,1)\) from \cref{eq:sqrt:root_range}.  The extreme requirements are
\begin{align}
  \paren{S_0 - aB^{-1}} - \rho B^{-1} &\le \frac12,\label{eq:sqrt:init_cover_1}\\
  S_0 + \rho B^{-1} &\ge 1.\label{eq:sqrt:init_cover_2}
\end{align}
The second inequality is immediate from \(S_0=1\).  In the first,
\[
  aB^{-1} + \rho B^{-1} = \rho,
\]
so \cref{eq:sqrt:init_cover_1} reduces to \(1-\rho \le \frac12\), again satisfied for
\(\rho \ge \frac12\).

The explicit startup intervals are:

\paragraph{Radix-2 \((B=2,\rho=1)\).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item If \(s_1=0\), then \(S_1=1\) and
    \[
      \frac12 \le \sqrt{X} \le \frac32
      \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
      \frac14 \le X \le \frac94.
    \]
  \item If \(s_1=-1\), then \(S_1=\frac12\) and
    \[
      0 \le \sqrt{X} \le 1
      \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
      0 \le X \le 1.
    \]
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Radix-4 \((B=4,\rho=\frac23)\).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item If \(s_1=0\), then \(S_1=1\) and
    \[
      \frac56 \le \sqrt{X} \le \frac76
      \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
      \frac{25}{36} \le X \le \frac{49}{36}.
    \]
  \item If \(s_1=-1\), then \(S_1=\frac34\) and
    \[
      \frac7{12} \le \sqrt{X} \le \frac{11}{12}
      \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
      \frac{49}{144} \le X \le \frac{121}{144}.
    \]
  \item If \(s_1=-2\), then \(S_1=\frac12\) and
    \[
      \frac13 \le \sqrt{X} \le \frac23
      \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
      \frac19 \le X \le \frac49.
    \]
\end{itemize}

\subsection{From exact intervals to a truncated selector}
\label{ssec:sqrt:truncated_selector}

Square root uses the same residual truncation idea as division, but the selector also needs a
truncated partial-root estimate.  Let \(t_j\) again denote the carry-propagated truncation of
\(BR_j\) with \(\alpha\) fractional bits, so
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:trunc_error}
  0 \le BR_j - t_j < 2^{-(\alpha-1)}.
\end{equation}
Define
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:sqrt:tau_def}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{2^\alpha t_j},\\
  \sigma_j &= \Int\paren{2^\beta S_{j,\text{trunc}}}.
\end{align}
As in division, the selector plane is finite: \((\sigma_j,\tau_j)\) ranges over finitely many cells.

The continuity condition is the square-root analogue of \cref{eq:division:continuity_positive}, but
now the exact boundaries depend on \(S_j\) and \(j\).  For adjacent digits \(k-1\) and \(k\), define
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:sqrt:UkLk}
  U_{k-1}(S_j,j)
  &= 2(k-1+\rho)S_j + (k-1+\rho)^2 B^{-(j+1)},\\
  L_k(S_j,j)
  &= 2(k-\rho)S_j + (k-\rho)^2 B^{-(j+1)}.
\end{align}
A sufficient continuity condition is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:continuity}
  \min_{S_j} \paren{U_{k-1}(S_j,j) - 2^{-(\alpha-1)}}
  \;\ge\;
  \max_{S_j} L_k(S_j,j).
\end{equation}
Later we will apply this formula with specific Radix-2 and Radix-4 coefficients.

\subsection{Worked example: Radix-2 square root}
\label{ssec:sqrt:radix2}

Take \(B=2\), \(a=1\), \(\rho=1\), and the digit set \(\{-1,0,1\}\).

\subsubsection{Exact selection intervals}
\label{sssec:sqrt:radix2_exact}

Insert \(B=2\) and \(\rho=1\) into \cref{eq:sqrt:exact_interval}:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_exact}
  s_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    1  & \text{if } 0 \le 2R_j \le 4S_j + 4\cdot 2^{-(j+1)},\\
    0  & \text{if } -2S_j + 2^{-(j+1)} \le 2R_j \le 2S_j + 2^{-(j+1)},\\
    -1 & \text{if } -4S_j + 4\cdot 2^{-(j+1)} \le 2R_j \le 0.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Generic selector and precision choice}
\label{sssec:sqrt:radix2_generic}

The selector uses
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_tau_sigma_defs}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{2^\alpha t_j},
  & \tau_j &\le 2^{\alpha+1}R_j < \tau_j + 2,\\
  \sigma_j &= \Int\paren{2^\beta S_{j,\text{trunc}}}.
\end{align}
For the generic path (that is, excluding the startup exceptions discussed next), the scripts use
\(\beta=2\) and a single partial-root bucket \(\sigma_j = 3\).

Apply \cref{eq:sqrt:continuity}.

\paragraph{Boundary between \(s=0\) and \(s=1\).}
Here \(k=1\).  Since \(\rho=1\),
\[
  U_0(S_j,j) = 2S_j + 2^{-(j+1)},\qquad L_1(S_j,j)=0.
\]
The positive constant \(2^{-(j+1)}\) only helps, so the simpler sufficient inequality is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_boundary_01}
  2\cdot 2^{-\beta}\sigma_j - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} \ge 0.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Boundary between \(s=-1\) and \(s=0\).}
Here \(k=0\), so
\[
  U_{-1}(S_j,j)=0,\qquad L_0(S_j,j) = -2S_j + 2^{-(j+1)}.
\]
Using the largest admissible \(S_j\) for a given \(\sigma_j\) gives the sufficient inequality
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_boundary_m10}
  2\cdot 2^{-\beta}\sigma_j - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} - 2^{-(j+1)} \ge 0.
\end{equation}

With the generic bucket \(\sigma_j=3\), the smallest choice satisfying both inequalities is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_alpha_beta}
  \alpha=2,\qquad \beta=2.
\end{equation}
Therefore
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_tau_sigma_values}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{4\,t_j},
  & \tau_j &\le 8R_j < \tau_j + 2,\\
  \sigma_j &= 3.
\end{align}
The corresponding basic selector is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_generic_selector}
  s_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    1  & \text{if } 0 \le \tau_j < 4(\sigma_j+1),\\
    0  & \text{if } -2(\sigma_j-1) \le \tau_j \le 2\sigma_j - 2,\\
    -1 & \text{if } -4(\sigma_j+1)-2 < \tau_j \le -2.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Startup path near \(X=\frac14\)}
\label{sssec:sqrt:radix2_near_quarter}

When \(X\) is close to \(\frac14\), initialization chooses \(s_1=-1\) and the following digits stay
zero for a while:
\[
  s_1=-1,\qquad s_2=s_3=\dotsb=s_j=0.
\]
Since \(S_1 = 1-\frac12 = \frac12\), the partial root remains
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_near_quarter_Sj}
  S_j = \frac12 \qquad (j\ge 1).
\end{equation}
Hence the truncated partial-root bucket is the minimum one:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_near_quarter_sigma}
  \sigma_j = \Int\paren{4\cdot 0.10} = 2.
\end{equation}

The initial residual is
\[
  R_1 = 2\paren{X-\frac14} \ge 0.
\]
Whenever the next digit is zero, the recurrence reduces to \(R_{k}=2R_{k-1}\), so the residual
stays nonnegative throughout this zero-digit prefix.  Since
\[
  0 \le 2R_j - t_j < 2^{-(\alpha-1)},
\]
we have \(t_j > -2^{-(\alpha-1)}\), and with \(\alpha=2\),
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_near_quarter_tau}
  \tau_j \ge -2.
\end{equation}
Therefore the negative-digit region is unreachable and the selector collapses to
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_near_quarter_selector}
  s_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & \text{if } 0 \le \tau_j < 4(\sigma_j+1),\\
    0 & \text{if } -2 \le \tau_j \le 2\sigma_j - 2,
  \end{cases}
  \qquad \sigma_j=2.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Startup path near \(X=1\)}
\label{sssec:sqrt:radix2_near_one}

When \(X\) is close to \(1\), the startup path is
\[
  s_1=s_2=\dotsb=s_j=0.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_near_one_Sj}
  S_j = 1
\end{equation}
throughout the zero-digit prefix.  Because \(X<1\),
\[
  R_1 = 2(X-1) < 0,
\]
and the zero-digit recurrence \(R_k=2R_{k-1}\) preserves that negativity.  Hence \(2R_j<0\), and
because truncation is one-sided, \(t_j < 0\) as well.  Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_near_one_tau}
  \tau_j \le -1.
\end{equation}
The truncated partial-root bucket is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_near_one_sigma}
  \sigma_j = \Int\paren{4\cdot 1.00} = 4.
\end{equation}
The positive-digit region is unreachable, so the selector reduces to
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix2_near_one_selector}
  s_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    0  & \text{if } -2(\sigma_j-1) \le \tau_j \le -1,\\
    -1 & \text{if } -4\sigma_j - 2 < \tau_j \le -2.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Worked example: Radix-4 square root}
\label{ssec:sqrt:radix4}

Take \(B=4\), \(a=2\), \(\rho=2/3\), and digit set \(\{-2,-1,0,1,2\}\).

\subsubsection{Exact selection intervals}
\label{sssec:sqrt:radix4_exact}

Substitute \(B=4\) and \(\rho=2/3\) into \cref{eq:sqrt:exact_interval}:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_exact}
  s_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    2  & \text{if }
      \frac83 S_j + \frac{16}{9}4^{-(j+1)} \le 4R_j \le
      \frac{16}{3}S_j + \frac{64}{9}4^{-(j+1)},\\[0.4em]
    1  & \text{if }
      \frac23 S_j + \frac19 4^{-(j+1)} \le 4R_j \le
      \frac{10}{3}S_j + \frac{25}{9}4^{-(j+1)},\\[0.4em]
    0  & \text{if }
      -\frac43 S_j + \frac49 4^{-(j+1)} \le 4R_j \le
      \frac43 S_j + \frac49 4^{-(j+1)},\\[0.4em]
    -1 & \text{if }
      -\frac{10}{3} S_j + \frac{25}{9}4^{-(j+1)} \le 4R_j \le
      -\frac23 S_j + \frac19 4^{-(j+1)},\\[0.4em]
    -2 & \text{if }
      -\frac{16}{3} S_j + \frac{64}{9}4^{-(j+1)} \le 4R_j \le
      -\frac83 S_j + \frac{16}{9}4^{-(j+1)}.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Generic selector for \(j\ge 2\)}
\label{sssec:sqrt:radix4_generic}

Use
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_tau_sigma_defs}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{2^\alpha t_j},
  & \tau_j &\le 2^{\alpha+2}R_j < \tau_j + 2,\\
  \sigma_j &= \Int\paren{2^\beta S_{j,\text{trunc}}}.
\end{align}
The scripts use \(\beta=4\), so the generic buckets are \(\sigma_j \in \{9,\dotsc,15\}\).
Apply \cref{eq:sqrt:continuity}.

\paragraph{Boundary between \(s=1\) and \(s=2\).}
Here \(k=2\), so
\[
  U_1(S_j,j) = \frac{10}{3}S_j + \frac{25}{9}4^{-(j+1)},\qquad
  L_2(S_j,j) = \frac83 S_j + \frac{16}{9}4^{-(j+1)}.
\]
Using the smallest \(S_j\) on the upper side and the largest \(S_j\) on the lower side, the constant
terms combine to the sufficient inequality
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_boundary_12}
  \frac23 2^{-\beta}\sigma_j - \frac83 2^{-\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} \ge 0.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Boundary between \(s=0\) and \(s=1\).}
This gives
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_boundary_01}
  \frac23 2^{-\beta}\sigma_j - \frac23 2^{-\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} \ge 0.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Boundary between \(s=-1\) and \(s=0\).}
Now the explicit \(4^{-(j+1)}\) term matters:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_boundary_m10}
  \frac23 2^{-\beta}\sigma_j - \frac23 2^{-\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} - \frac49 4^{-(j+1)} \ge 0.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Boundary between \(s=-2\) and \(s=-1\).}
This is the tightest one:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_boundary_m2m1}
  \frac23 2^{-\beta}\sigma_j - \frac83 2^{-\beta} - 2^{-(\alpha-1)} - \frac{25}{9}4^{-(j+1)} \ge 0.
\end{equation}

Choose \(\beta=4\) and try \(\alpha=4\).  At the worst generic bucket \(\sigma_j=9\),
\begin{align*}
  \frac23 2^{-4}\sigma_j - \frac83 2^{-4} - 2^{-(4-1)}
  &= \frac23\cdot \frac9{16} - \frac83\cdot \frac1{16} - \frac18\\
  &= \frac38 - \frac16 - \frac18\\
  &= \frac1{12}.
\end{align*}
So \cref{eq:sqrt:radix4_boundary_m2m1} becomes
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_j_restriction}
  \frac1{12} - \frac{25}{9}4^{-(j+1)} \ge 0.
\end{equation}
For \(j=1\), the left-hand side is
\[
  \frac1{12} - \frac{25}{9}\cdot \frac1{16}
  = \frac{4}{48} - \frac{25}{144}
  = -\frac{13}{144} < 0.
\]
For \(j=2\), it is
\[
  \frac1{12} - \frac{25}{9}\cdot \frac1{64}
  = \frac{16}{192} - \frac{25}{576}
  = \frac{23}{576} > 0.
\]
Therefore the generic \((\alpha,\beta)=(4,4)\) selector is safe from \(j\ge 2\) onward.

With that choice,
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_tau_sigma_values}
  \tau_j &= \Int\paren{16\,t_j},
  & \tau_j &\le 64R_j < \tau_j + 2,\\
  \sigma_j &\in \set{9,\dotsc,15}.
\end{align}
The resulting basic selector for \(j\ge 2\) is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_generic_selector}
  s_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    2  & \text{if } \frac83(\sigma_j+1) \le \tau_j < \frac{16}{3}(\sigma_j+1),\\
    1  & \text{if } \frac23(\sigma_j+1) \le \tau_j \le \frac{10}{3}\sigma_j - 2,\\
    0  & \text{if } -\frac43\paren{\sigma_j - \frac1{12}} \le \tau_j \le \frac43\sigma_j - 2,\\
    -1 & \text{if } -\frac{10}{3}\paren{\sigma_j - \frac5{24}} \le \tau_j \le -\frac23(\sigma_j+1) - 2,\\
    -2 & \text{if } -\frac{16}{3}(\sigma_j+1)-2 < \tau_j \le -\frac83(\sigma_j+1)-2.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Startup path near \(X=\frac14\)}
\label{sssec:sqrt:radix4_near_quarter}

When \(X\) is close to \(\frac14\), initialization chooses \(s_1=-2\) and then stays at zero for a
while:
\[
  s_1=-2,\qquad s_2=s_3=\dotsb=s_j=0.
\]
Since \(S_1 = 1 - \frac24 = \frac12\), the partial root remains
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_near_quarter_Sj}
  S_j = \frac12 \qquad (j\ge 1),
\end{equation}
so
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_near_quarter_sigma}
  \sigma_j = \Int\paren{16\cdot 0.1000} = 8.
\end{equation}

The initial residual is
\[
  R_1 = 4\paren{X-\frac14} \ge 0,
\]
and zero digits preserve that nonnegativity.  Since
\[
  0 \le 4R_j - t_j < 2^{-(\alpha-1)},
\]
we have \(t_j > -2^{-(\alpha-1)}\).  With \(\alpha=4\), that implies
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_near_quarter_tau}
  \tau_j \ge -2.
\end{equation}
So the negative-side overlap cells are unreachable, and the selector reduces to
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_near_quarter_selector}
  s_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    2 & \text{if } \frac83(\sigma_j+1) \le \tau_j < \frac{16}{3}(\sigma_j+1),\\
    1 & \text{if } \frac23(\sigma_j+1) \le \tau_j \le \frac{10}{3}\sigma_j - 2,\\
    0 & \text{if } -2 \le \tau_j \le \frac43\sigma_j - 2,
  \end{cases}
  \qquad \sigma_j=8.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Startup path near \(X=1\)}
\label{sssec:sqrt:radix4_near_one}

When \(X\) is close to \(1\), the startup path is
\[
  s_1=s_2=\dotsb=s_j=0,
\]
so
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_near_one_Sj}
  S_j = 1.
\end{equation}
Again \(R_1 = 4(X-1) < 0\), and zero digits preserve the sign.  Therefore \(4R_j<0\), and the
one-sided truncation implies
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_near_one_tau}
  \tau_j \le -1.
\end{equation}
The partial-root bucket is now
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_near_one_sigma}
  \sigma_j = \Int\paren{16\cdot 1.0000} = 16.
\end{equation}

Re-evaluate the restrictive continuity inequality \cref{eq:sqrt:radix4_boundary_m2m1} at
\(\sigma_j=16\), \(\alpha=4\), and \(\beta=4\):
\begin{align}
  \frac23 2^{-4}\cdot 16 - \frac83 2^{-4} - 2^{-(4-1)} - \frac{25}{9}4^{-(j+1)}
  &= \frac23 - \frac16 - \frac18 - \frac{25}{9}4^{-(j+1)} \notag\\
  &= \frac38 - \frac{25}{9}4^{-(j+1)}. \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_near_one_continuity}
\end{align}
For \(j=1\),
\[
  \frac38 - \frac{25}{9}\cdot \frac1{16}
  = \frac{54}{144} - \frac{25}{144}
  = \frac{29}{144} > 0.
\]
So this path is already safe from the first standard post-initialization iteration onward.

Because \(\tau_j\le -1\), the positive-side selector cells are unreachable.  The reduced selector is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:sqrt:radix4_near_one_selector}
  s_{j+1} =
  \begin{cases}
    0  & \text{if } -\frac43\paren{\sigma_j-\frac13} \le \tau_j \le -1,\\
    -1 & \text{if } -\frac{10}{3}\paren{\sigma_j-\frac56} \le \tau_j \le -\frac23\sigma_j - 2,\\
    -2 & \text{if } -\frac{16}{3}\sigma_j - 2 < \tau_j \le -\frac83\sigma_j - 2.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\section{Practical summary}
\label{sec:summary}

The complete design workflow is the same for division and square root.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Choose a radix \(B\) and a redundant digit set \(\{-a,\dotsc,a\}\), hence \(\rho=a/(B-1)\).
  \item Derive the exact recurrence for the scaled residual \(R_j\).
  \item Derive the exact selection intervals that guarantee the next residual remains contained.
  \item Introduce truncated selector variables \(\tau_j\) and \(\delta\) or \(\sigma_j\).
  \item Use a continuity condition to choose \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) large enough that adjacent digit
        regions still overlap after truncation.
  \item Enumerate the finite selector grid and record which digits are legal in each cell.
  \item Either:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item keep the overlap cells explicit (basic selector), or
      \item assign each overlap cell to one digit using a deterministic boundary rule (optimized
        selector).
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

Division is the easier of the two problems because the selection boundaries depend only on the
divisor \(D\).  Square root is harder because the boundaries depend on the evolving partial root
\(S_j\) and on iteration-dependent \(B^{-(j+1)}\) terms.  Those extra terms are precisely why the
Radix-4 square-root example needs separate startup-path analysis.

\newpage

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Digit-recurrence arithmetic is built on a simple but powerful idea: use a redundant digit set so
that adjacent digits overlap, and then exploit that overlap to perform fast selection from truncated
state.  The full-width exact residual is never required on the selector critical path.

In this tutorial we:
\begin{itemize}
  \item derived the exact SRT-style recurrences for division and square root,
  \item proved the residual containment conditions from the infinite signed-digit expansions,
  \item converted the exact inequalities into practical truncated selectors,
  \item worked through Radix-2 and Radix-4 division,
  \item worked through Radix-2 and Radix-4 square root, including the startup special cases, and
  \item explained how the optimized selectors arise from deterministic overlap resolution.
\end{itemize}

The central design trade-off is clear.  A higher radix can produce more result bits per iteration,
but it typically requires more selector precision and more complicated multiples.  More redundancy
widens the legal overlap and simplifies selection, but it also enlarges the datapath work associated
with each selected digit.  SRT design is therefore not only about algebra; it is about choosing a
comfortable operating point in this three-way trade-off among radix, redundancy, and selector
complexity.

\appendix

\section{Deterministic overlap resolution used by the optimized Radix-4 scripts}
\label{sec:appendix_transform}

The basic selectors leave overlap cells visible.  The optimized Radix-4 scripts replace each overlap
interval by a single split point chosen to land on logic-friendly modulo boundaries.  This appendix
summarizes the exact rule implemented in the repository's \texttt{transform} helper.

Suppose two adjacent digits have the interval endpoints
\[
  \text{lower digit upper bound } = x,\qquad
  \text{higher digit lower bound } = y,
\]
with \(x \ge y\).  The overlap width is measured by
\[
  \mathrm{diff} = x-y.
\]
The scripts replace the original pair \((x,y)\) by a snapped pair \((x',y')\) with \(x' < y'\),
thereby resolving the overlap to a single boundary.

\paragraph{Case 1: zero-width overlap (\(\mathrm{diff}=0\)).}
If the shared boundary is odd, choose the pair \((x,x+1)\).  If it is even, choose
\((x-1,x)\).  This pushes the final split to an odd/even boundary that is easier to encode.

\paragraph{Case 2: small overlap (\(1 \le \mathrm{diff} \le 2\)).}
Use modulus \(M=4\).  Let
\[
  \text{base} = \floor{\frac{y-1}{4}}\cdot 4.
\]
Then the scripts choose either
\[
  (x',y') = \paren{\text{base}+1,\ \text{base}+2}
\]
or
\[
  (x',y') = \paren{\text{base}+3,\ \text{base}+4},
\]
depending on whether the original upper endpoint \(x\) lies below or above the threshold
\(\text{base}+3\).

\paragraph{Case 3: medium overlap (\(3 \le \mathrm{diff} \le 6\)).}
Use modulus \(M=8\).  With
\[
  \text{base} = \floor{\frac{y-1}{8}}\cdot 8,
\]
the snapped pair is either
\[
  (x',y') = \paren{\text{base}+3,\ \text{base}+4}
\]
or
\[
  (x',y') = \paren{\text{base}+7,\ \text{base}+8},
\]
again depending on whether \(x\) is below or above the threshold \(\text{base}+7\).

\paragraph{Case 4: large overlap (\(7 \le \mathrm{diff} \le 14\)).}
The same rule is applied with modulus \(M=16\), which snaps the split to a
\((16a+7,16a+8)\)-type boundary.

In code form, the rule is:
\begin{quote}\ttfamily
diff = x - y \\
if diff == 0: snap to (x,x+1) or (x-1,x) depending on parity \\
elif 1 <= diff <= 2: use M = 4 \\
elif 3 <= diff <= 6: use M = 8 \\
elif 7 <= diff <= 14: use M = 16 \\
base = floor((y-1)/M) * M \\
threshold = base + M - 1 \\
if x < threshold: choose (base + M/2 - 1,\ base + M/2) \\
else: choose (base + M - 1,\ base + M)
\end{quote}

The important point is not the specific integers themselves, but the design intent: overlap is
resolved in a deterministic way that preserves correctness while nudging the final boundaries onto
simple modulo classes.  That is why the optimized selector plots look cleaner than the basic plots.
